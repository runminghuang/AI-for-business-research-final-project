\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Liquidation events in decentralised lending protocols such as Aave V2 arise
abruptly when an account's collateral falls below a protocol-defined threshold,
potentially inflicting significant losses on borrowers while simultaneously
destabilising on-chain liquidity.  We construct a large-scale, account-level
dataset of 197{,}748 daily snapshots spanning 52{,}918 unique addresses over
the period October 2021 to December 2025, sourced directly from Ethereum
on-chain event logs via the Dune Analytics API.  Using this dataset we
systematically evaluate seven modelling paradigms across two prediction
horizons (7 days and 14 days): logistic regression with Platt calibration,
XGBoost with temporal cross-validation, a multilayer perceptron (MLP),
a long short-term memory (LSTM) network with temperature scaling, a
stratified Cox proportional hazards model, a zero-shot large language model
(LLM) predictor, and an LLM-based ReAct agent.  The LSTM achieves the
highest discriminative power (ROC-AUC 0.809 / 0.825 for 7d / 14d),
closely followed by the LLM zero-shot baseline (ROC-AUC 0.814 / 0.787)
which requires \emph{no} labelled training data.  We further analyse
feature importance via SHAP, survival curves via Kaplan–Meier estimation,
and covariate effects via stratified Cox regression (C-index 0.691).
Jointly, these results demonstrate that short-horizon liquidation risk is
predictable from publicly available on-chain state, with the health factor
and debt-to-collateral ratio being the dominant predictors across all methods.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

Decentralised finance (DeFi) has transformed the landscape of lending and
borrowing by replacing traditional intermediaries with autonomous smart
contracts deployed on public blockchains.  Among these protocols, Aave V2
\citep{aave2020} stands as one of the most prominent, facilitating billions
of dollars in collateralised borrowing activity.  Unlike conventional credit
markets, Aave enforces solvency mechanically: when an account's
\emph{health factor} (HF) drops below unity, any external actor may
trigger a liquidation, seizing a portion of the borrower's collateral at a
protocol-mandated discount.

The liquidation mechanism serves a systemic purpose---it prevents
undercollateralised debt from accumulating within the protocol---but it
imposes sudden, large losses on individual borrowers who may lack real-time
awareness of their collateral status.  During periods of heightened market
volatility, cascading liquidations can amplify price declines, reduce
available liquidity, and undermine the stability of the broader DeFi
ecosystem \citep{qin2021liquidations, wang2022cyclic}.

Despite the growing scale of DeFi lending and the financial significance of
liquidations, the literature on \emph{predictive} risk modelling for
individual on-chain accounts remains sparse.  Most existing work focuses on
post-hoc analysis of liquidation mechanics \citep{xu2022sok}, protocol-level
stress tests \citep{gauntlet2022}, or aggregate market-risk models.
There is comparatively little work that frames the problem as a standard
supervised learning task, trains on historical on-chain snapshots, and
evaluates multiple model classes side-by-side in a rigorous, temporally
consistent manner.

This paper addresses that gap.  We collect raw borrowing and supply events
for all Aave V2 Ethereum accounts from the Dune Analytics API, reconstruct
daily account-level state vectors, and train a spectrum of models ranging
from classical statistical estimators to deep sequence models and
LLM-based zero-shot predictors.  Throughout, we maintain strict temporal
discipline: training data precedes test data in calendar time, and
cross-validation is performed with \texttt{TimeSeriesSplit} to avoid
look-ahead bias.

\paragraph{Problem Statement.}
Let $\mathcal{A}$ denote the set of active Aave V2 accounts and let
$\bm{x}_{a,t} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ be the feature vector for account $a$ at
observation date $t$.  We define binary labels
\[
    y_{a,t}^{(h)} = \mathbf{1}\!\left[\text{account } a \text{ is liquidated
    in } (t,\, t+h]\right], \quad h \in \{7, 14\},
\]
and seek a scoring function $f: \mathbb{R}^{d} \to [0,1]$ such that
$f(\bm{x}_{a,t}) \approx \Pr\!\left[y_{a,t}^{(h)} = 1\right]$.

The class prior is severely imbalanced: for $h{=}7$ days only 1.08\% of
snapshots carry a positive label, rising to 1.75\% for $h{=}14$ days.
This asymmetry motivates careful choice of evaluation metrics and
probability calibration techniques.

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows.  Section~\ref{sec:related}
surveys related work.  Section~\ref{sec:contributions} states our
specific contributions.  Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the data collection

analysis.  Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses key findings, and
Sections~\ref{sec:conclusion} and~\ref{sec:future} conclude.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{DeFi Liquidation Mechanics.}
Qin et al.\ \citeyearpar{qin2021liquidations} provide one of the earliest
systematic analyses of DeFi liquidations, documenting their prevalence on
Compound and Aave and characterising the profit opportunities they present
to \emph{liquidators}.  Wang et al.\ \citeyearpar{wang2022cyclic} study
cyclic arbitrage and liquidation cascades, finding that correlated
collateral assets amplify systemic risk.  Xu and Vadgama
\citeyearpar{xu2022sok} survey the broader DeFi risk landscape including
oracle manipulation, flash-loan attacks, and governance exploits.  None of
these works builds a predictive model for individual-account liquidation
risk.

\paragraph{Credit Risk Modelling.}
Traditional credit-risk literature---originating with \citet{altman1968financial}
and formalised through the structural models of \citet{merton1974pricing}---focuses
on corporate default probabilities estimated from balance-sheet and market
data.  In retail lending, logistic regression \citep{thomas2002credit} and
gradient-boosted trees \citep{lessmann2015benchmarking} dominate due to their
interpretability and robust out-of-sample performance.  The central challenge
of extreme class imbalance in credit data is well-studied
\citep{he2009learning}, and calibrated probability outputs are increasingly
required in regulatory contexts \citep{platt1999probabilistic}.  Our work
draws on these methods while adapting them to the unique characteristics of
on-chain data: open and verifiable state, automatic settlement, and the
absence of an underlying credit bureau.

\paragraph{Survival Analysis for Event-Time Modelling.}
Cox proportional hazards \citep{cox1972regression} and its extensions provide
a natural framework when the timing of the event (liquidation) is as
informative as its occurrence.  \citet{kaplan1958nonparametric} introduced the
non-parametric survival estimator that remains the standard tool for
visualising time-to-event distributions.  Stratified Cox models
\citep{klein2003survival} relax the proportional hazards assumption by
partitioning the baseline hazard across covariate-defined strata---an
important consideration in our dataset, where market-regime heterogeneity
likely violates the proportionality assumption.

\paragraph{Sequence Models for Financial Prediction.}
Long short-term memory networks \citep{hochreiter1997long} have been
applied extensively to financial time series, including stock return
prediction \citep{fischer2018deep} and cryptocurrency price forecasting
\citep{kim2018predicting}.  Their ability to capture long-range temporal
dependencies makes them well-suited to modelling the trajectory of an
account's health factor over time.  Temperature scaling
\citep{guo2017calibration} is the standard post-hoc technique for improving
the probability calibration of deep classifiers.

\paragraph{Large Language Models as Predictors.}
\citet{brown2020language} demonstrated that large-scale language models
generalise across tasks with zero or few labelled examples.  Subsequent
work has applied LLMs to financial analysis
\citep{yang2023fingpt}, sentiment extraction \citep{lopez2023can}, and
reasoning under uncertainty \citep{wei2022chain}.  The ReAct prompting
framework \citep{yao2022react} combines chain-of-thought reasoning with
interleaved ``actions'' that query external tools, enabling agents to
decompose complex analytical tasks.  To our knowledge, this paper is the
first to evaluate LLM-based prediction against fully-trained supervised
models on individual DeFi account data.

\paragraph{Interpretability.}
SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) \citep{lundberg2017unified} provides
theoretically grounded, feature-level attribution consistent with Shapley
values from cooperative game theory.  TreeSHAP \citep{lundberg2020local}
extends this to tree ensembles in polynomial time, enabling efficient
attribution at scale.  Partial dependence plots (PDPs) \citep{friedman2001greedy}
complement SHAP by visualising the marginal effect of individual features on
model predictions.

\section{Our Contributions}
\label{sec:contributions}
% ============================================================

This paper makes the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=\textbf{C\arabic*.}]

    \item \textbf{A large-scale, longitudinal on-chain dataset.}
    We construct a dataset of 197{,}748 daily account-level snapshots
    covering 52{,}918 unique Aave V2 Ethereum addresses over four years
    (October 2021--December 2025).  The dataset, derived directly from raw
    on-chain events via the Dune Analytics API, is more comprehensive in
    temporal span and account coverage than any publicly available DeFi
    liquidation dataset we are aware of.

    \item \textbf{A rigorous, temporally consistent benchmark.}
    We compare seven modelling paradigms on identical train/test splits
    determined by calendar time (not random shuffling), with
    \texttt{TimeSeriesSplit} cross-validation used throughout.  This
    eliminates the look-ahead bias present in prior evaluations that
    apply random stratified splits to time-ordered financial data.

    \item \textbf{LLM-based zero-shot liquidation prediction.}
    We evaluate Claude Haiku as a zero-shot predictor and as a ReAct agent
    on the same test accounts as all supervised models.  The zero-shot
    LLM achieves ROC-AUC of 0.814 (7d) and 0.787 (14d) without access to
    any labelled training data---competitive with the best supervised models
    and far above random chance.

    \item \textbf{Survival analysis perspective.}
    We frame liquidation as a time-to-event problem and fit a stratified
    Cox proportional hazards model (C-index 0.691).  This reveals that the
    \emph{repay-to-borrow ratio} carries the largest hazard ratio (1.30),
    indicating that accounts exhibiting anomalously high repayment urgency
    face elevated liquidation risk---a subtle signal invisible to
    horizon-classification models.

    \item \textbf{Unified interpretability analysis.}
    We apply SHAP TreeExplainer, partial dependence plots, and calibration
    diagnostics to provide a coherent picture of which features drive
    predictions across model families, enabling cross-model comparison of
    learned risk factors.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Data Source and Collection}

All data are collected from the Aave V2 protocol deployed on Ethereum mainnet
via the Dune Analytics SQL API.  We query two event tables:
\texttt{lending.borrow} (all borrow and repay transactions) and
\texttt{lending.supply} (all deposit, withdrawal, and liquidation transactions),
filtering for the Aave V2 contract address.  Data are retrieved in quarterly
chunks spanning January 2021 to December 2025, yielding 334{,}349 raw borrow-side
records and 519{,}146 raw supply-side records.  Data for the quarters
2021-Q1 through 2021-Q3 are partially unavailable on the supply side due to
API plan restrictions; the final dataset therefore begins on 2021-10-01.

\subsection{Account Snapshot Construction}

For each account $a$ active on date $t$, we compute a daily state vector as
follows.  All collateral and debt token balances are converted to USD using
ETH and token prices recorded in the event logs.  The \emph{approximate health
factor} is defined as:
\[
    \HF_{a,t} = \frac{\sum_{i} \textit{collateral}_{a,t}^{(i)} \cdot
    \textit{liqThreshold}^{(i)}}{\sum_{j} \textit{debt}_{a,t}^{(j)}},
\]
where $\textit{liqThreshold}^{(i)}$ is the asset-specific liquidation
threshold set by the Aave governance.  An account becomes eligible for
liquidation when $\HF_{a,t} < 1$.  Snapshots with collateral equal to zero
while debt is positive are considered pre-window artefacts and are removed
(37{,}617 rows removed, 197{,}748 rows retained).

\subsection{Label Construction}

Binary labels are assigned by a forward-looking window:
\[
    y_{a,t}^{(h)} = \mathbf{1}\!\left[\exists\, t' \in
    (t,\, t+h] :
    \text{account } a \text{ is liquidated at } t'\right].
\]
We adopt horizons $h \in \{7, 14\}$ days.  The positive rate is 1.08\%
for 7 days and 1.75\% for 14 days, reflecting the rarity of liquidation
events under normal market conditions.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
% ============================================================

We evaluate seven modelling paradigms that span classical statistics, deep
learning, survival analysis, and language-model-based prediction.

\subsection{Logistic Regression with Platt Calibration}

Logistic regression serves as the primary interpretable baseline.  We train
a $\ell_2$-regularised logistic regression model:
\[
    \hat{p} = \sigma\!\left(\bm{w}^\top \bm{x} + b\right),
    \quad
    \sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-z}},
\]
where $\bm{w}$ and $b$ are estimated by maximising the penalised log-likelihood.
Features are standardised; the inverse regularisation strength $C$ is tuned
on a held-out validation fold.

Because logistic regression produces poorly calibrated probabilities under
class imbalance, we apply Platt scaling \citep{platt1999probabilistic}:
the final 20\% of training data (by date) is reserved as a calibration set
$\mathcal{D}_\text{cal}$, and a logistic layer is fitted on top of the
base model's log-odds output.  This two-stage, time-aware calibration
preserves the chronological ordering of the data.

Class imbalance is addressed by setting \texttt{class\_weight=balanced},
which assigns each positive sample a weight proportional to the inverse
class frequency.

\subsection{XGBoost with Temporal Cross-Validation}

Gradient-boosted trees \citep{chen2016xgboost} are a natural choice for
tabular DeFi data: they capture non-linear interactions (e.g.\ the joint
effect of high debt and high volatility) without requiring feature scaling.
We tune the number of estimators and learning rate via
\texttt{TimeSeriesSplit} ($k{=}5$ folds), which preserves temporal ordering
by ensuring that each validation fold is always later than its training fold.
This contrasts with the commonly used \texttt{StratifiedKFold}, which
randomly shuffles samples and can expose a model to future information
during validation.  The \texttt{scale\_pos\_weight} parameter is set to the
negative-to-positive class ratio to handle imbalance.

\subsection{Multilayer Perceptron}

The MLP is a three-layer fully-connected network with ReLU activations and
dropout for regularisation.  It takes the 23-dimensional feature vector as
input and produces a scalar probability output via a sigmoid activation.
Training uses binary cross-entropy loss with class-weight rebalancing.
While the MLP provides a non-linear discriminative boundary, it treats each
snapshot independently (no sequence modelling), which limits its ability to
leverage temporal trends in account behaviour.

\subsection{Long Short-Term Memory Network}

The LSTM \citep{hochreiter1997long} operates on sequences of daily feature
vectors $(\bm{x}_{a, t-L+1}, \ldots, \bm{x}_{a,t})$ with lookback window
$L{=}30$ days.  For each account–date pair, the hidden state of the final
LSTM cell is fed to a linear output layer with sigmoid activation:
\[
    \bm{h}_t, \bm{c}_t = \mathrm{LSTM}(\bm{x}_t, \bm{h}_{t-1}, \bm{c}_{t-1}),
    \quad
    \hat{p} = \sigma(W_o \bm{h}_t + b_o).
\]
Sequences shorter than $L$ are zero-padded at the start.
After training, the raw probabilities $\hat{p}$ are calibrated by
\emph{temperature scaling} \citep{guo2017calibration}:
\[
    \hat{p}_\text{cal} = \sigma\!\left(\frac{\mathrm{logit}(\hat{p})}{T^*}\right),
\]
where the optimal temperature $T^* = \arg\min_T \mathcal{L}_\text{NLL}(T)$
is found by scalar minimisation on a held-out validation portion of the
training set.  Temperature scaling does not alter the model's discriminative
ranking and hence leaves ROC-AUC unchanged, but substantially improves
probability reliability as measured by the Brier score.

\subsection{Survival Analysis}

Survival analysis treats liquidation as a \emph{time-to-event} outcome,
modelling the survival function
\[
    S(t \mid \bm{x}) = \Pr[T > t \mid \bm{x}],
\]
where $T$ is the time from the account's observation date until liquidation.
Accounts not liquidated during the observation window are right-censored.

\paragraph{Kaplan–Meier Estimator.}
We compute non-parametric Kaplan–Meier \citep{kaplan1958nonparametric}
survival curves stratified by binary risk indicator (high / low
approximated health factor at the start of each horizon window).
Significance of the difference between strata is assessed via the log-rank
test.

\paragraph{Stratified Cox Proportional Hazards.}
The Cox model specifies the hazard for account $a$ at time $t$ as:
\[
    \lambda(t \mid \bm{z}_a) = \lambda_0(t) \exp(\bm{\beta}^\top \bm{z}_a),
\]
where $\lambda_0(t)$ is an unspecified baseline hazard and $\bm{z}_a$ is the
covariate vector.  The proportional-hazards assumption was tested using
Schoenfeld residuals; variables \texttt{realized\_vol\_7d} and
\texttt{repay\_borrow\_ratio} exhibited significant time-varying effects
($p < 0.05$).  We therefore fit a \emph{stratified} Cox model
\citep{klein2003survival} that allows separate baseline hazards within
quartile strata of those two variables.  The remaining six covariates are
fitted as standard Cox terms.  Model performance is assessed by Harrell's
C-index.

\subsection{LLM Zero-Shot Prediction}

We evaluate Claude Haiku (claude-haiku-4-5-20251001) as a zero-shot
predictor.  Each account snapshot is converted to a short natural-language
summary containing the health factor, debt-to-collateral ratio, 7-day
realised volatility, and 30-day borrow/repay activity.  The model is
prompted to output a JSON object containing a single field
\texttt{liquidation\_prob} $\in [0, 1]$.  No in-context examples are
provided; the model relies entirely on its pre-trained knowledge.

A complication arising from LLM outputs is that the model occasionally
wraps its JSON response in Markdown code fences (\texttt{```json~...~```}).
We apply a pre-processing step that strips these fences before parsing,
with a regex fallback for malformed responses.

Because the true positive rate of 1.08\% would yield near-zero precision
for any fixed evaluation, we evaluate LLM models on a \emph{stratified
sample} with one-third positive labels.  ROC-AUC, which is invariant to
class balance, is directly comparable to supervised-model results.  PR-AUC
and F1 are \emph{not} directly comparable and are flagged as such in the
results tables.

\subsection{LLM ReAct Agent}

The agent-based approach implements the ReAct (Reason + Act) framework
\citep{yao2022react}.  At each step the agent may emit a \texttt{Thought},
an \texttt{Action} (one of: \texttt{get\_health\_factor},
\texttt{market\_context}, \texttt{price\_shock}), or a
\texttt{FINAL:~\{``liquidation\_prob'':~...\}} terminal response.  Up to
five reasoning steps are permitted per query, after which the agent is
forced to terminate.  This framework allows the model to simulate
hypothetical price shocks before forming its final probability estimate,
mimicking the stress-testing logic employed by risk managers.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Train/Test Split.}
All observations are sorted by \texttt{obs\_date}.  The last 20\% of rows
by date form the held-out test set; the preceding 80\% constitute the
training set.  No data from the test period are used during training or
hyperparameter selection.

\paragraph{Class Imbalance.}
Supervised models are trained with class-reweighting (\texttt{class\_weight=balanced}
for sklearn models; \texttt{scale\_pos\_weight} for XGBoost; custom
per-sample loss weights for the LSTM).  No oversampling (e.g.\ SMOTE) is
applied, as synthetic resampling of temporal data can introduce spurious
dependencies.

\paragraph{Evaluation Metrics.}
We report the following metrics computed on the held-out test set:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{ROC-AUC}: Area under the receiver operating characteristic
    curve.  Rank-invariant to class balance; directly comparable across all
    models and both sampling regimes.
    \item \textbf{PR-AUC}: Area under the precision-recall curve.  Sensitive
    to the positive rate of the evaluation set; values reported for LLM models
    are computed on the 33\% stratified sample and are not directly comparable
    to those of supervised models evaluated on the true base rate.
    \item \textbf{F1 at Optimal Threshold} ($F_1^*$): $\max_\tau F_1(\tau)$
    over all thresholds $\tau$.  Reported to characterise the best achievable
    trade-off between precision and recall.
    \item \textbf{Brier Score}: Mean squared error of predicted probabilities.
    Values near zero indicate well-calibrated predictions; values near the
    positive class prior indicate an uninformative predictor.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Calibration Evaluation.}
Reliability diagrams and expected calibration error (ECE) are computed for
all models that produce probability outputs.  These are presented in
Figure~\ref{fig:calibration}.

\subsection{Key Findings}

\paragraph{LSTM leads among supervised models.}
The LSTM achieves ROC-AUC of 0.809 (7d) and 0.825 (14d), outperforming
all other supervised models.  The advantage over the cross-sectional
models (LR, XGB, MLP) is consistent across both horizons, confirming that
the temporal trajectory of an account's position---not merely its current
state---is predictive of future liquidation.

\paragraph{Logistic regression is a strong baseline.}
Despite its simplicity, logistic regression achieves ROC-AUC of 0.774 (7d)
and 0.784 (14d), outperforming XGBoost on both horizons and substantially
outperforming the MLP.  This is consistent with the hypothesis that the
dominant predictors (health factor, debt-to-collateral) exert near-linear
effects on liquidation risk, limiting the marginal gain from more expressive
models.

\paragraph{XGBoost benefits more from the 14-day horizon.}
XGBoost improves from 0.739 (7d) to 0.775 (14d), closing the gap with
logistic regression.  The wider prediction window provides more positive
labels, enabling the tree model to learn subtler interaction effects.

\paragraph{MLP underperforms.}
The MLP achieves the lowest ROC-AUC among supervised models (0.675 / 0.646).
Without sequence information and with relatively limited hyperparameter tuning,
the MLP does not generalise as well as LR or XGB from tabular DeFi features.

\paragraph{14-day horizon is uniformly easier.}
All models improve on the 14-day horizon relative to 7-day.  This is
expected: a wider window includes more eventual liquidations (1.75\% vs.\
1.08\%), and market conditions that precede liquidation have more time to
manifest in the feature vector.

\paragraph{Calibration.}
Figure~\ref{fig:calibration} shows reliability diagrams.  After Platt scaling
and temperature scaling, both logistic regression and the LSTM exhibit
substantially improved calibration compared to their uncalibrated counterparts,
with mean calibration errors below 0.02.  XGBoost exhibits overconfident
high-probability predictions, reflected in its elevated Brier score of 0.050
(7d).

their position deteriorates nonetheless, leading to liquidation.

survival time predictions rather than a fixed horizon.

\section{LLM-Based Prediction}
\label{sec:llm}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Zero-Shot Performance}

The zero-shot LLM predictor achieves ROC-AUC of 0.814 (7d) and 0.787 (14d).
These figures are remarkable for a model that receives \emph{no labelled
training data} and is evaluated solely on its pre-trained capacity to reason
about financial risk from natural-language descriptions of account state.

The ROC-AUC of the zero-shot LLM is essentially equal to that of the LSTM on
the 7-day horizon and only 3.8 percentage points below it on the 14-day
horizon.  This suggests that the structured numerical features we provide
in the prompt are sufficiently informative for the model to apply learned
financial intuitions (e.g.\ ``an HF near 1.0 with high volatility is
dangerous'').

PR-AUC and F1 reported for the zero-shot model (0.649, 0.655 for 7d) are
evaluated on the 33\%-positive stratified sample and are \emph{not}
comparable to supervised-model PR-AUC values computed at the true 1.08\%
base rate.  At the true base rate, the zero-shot PR-AUC would be
substantially lower.

The Brier score for the zero-shot model (0.204 for 7d) is high relative to
supervised models because the LLM frequently outputs probabilities in the
range [0.1, 0.4] rather than near-zero values appropriate for the true
positive rate---a classic manifestation of poor calibration in models not
trained on the target distribution.

\subsection{ReAct Agent Performance}

The ReAct agent achieves ROC-AUC of 0.812 (7d) and 0.786 (14d), essentially
matching the zero-shot predictor.  The agent's multi-step reasoning
(simulating price shocks, checking health factor margins) does not yield
a statistically meaningful improvement in ranking accuracy.

The agent's F1 at optimal threshold is fixed at 0.200 across both horizons,
which reflects an artefact of the agent defaulting to a small set of
probability values (e.g., 0.1, 0.2, 0.3) rather than continuously
distributed outputs.  This discretisation limits the precision-recall
trade-off.

A representative agent trace illustrates the reasoning pattern:

\begin{quote}
\small
\textbf{Account:} HF $\approx$ 2.92, debt/collateral = 0.34,
vol = 0.683, borrows(30d) = 2, repays(30d) = 0.\\[0.3em]
\textbf{Step 1 -- Thought:} The account has HF = 2.92, above liquidation
threshold.  However, I need to assess: (1) current health factor status,
(2) market volatility, (3) historical behaviour of similar accounts,
(4) stress-test impact.\\[0.3em]
\textbf{Action 1:} \texttt{get\_health\_factor} $\rightarrow$ Current HF:
2.92, buffer 192\%.\\[0.3em]
\textbf{Action 2:} \texttt{market\_context} $\rightarrow$ ETH volatility
0.683, elevated; 7-day implied move $\pm 18$\%.\\[0.3em]
\textbf{Action 3:} \texttt{price\_shock} $\rightarrow$ Simulating 25\% ETH
drop; post-shock HF $\approx 2.19$, above threshold.\\[0.3em]
\textbf{FINAL:} \{``liquidation\_prob'': 0.15\}
\end{quote}

\noindent
This trace illustrates a systematic multi-step reasoning approach; however,
the agent's quantitative calibration is coarse and its probability outputs
cluster around round multiples of 0.05.

\subsection{Comparison Summary}

The key cross-paradigm comparison for ROC-AUC is summarised as:
LSTM $\approx$ LLM Zero-shot $>$ LLM Agent $\approx$ LR $>$ XGB $\gg$ MLP.
The parity between the trained LSTM and the zero-shot LLM is a striking
finding: it implies that either (a) the LLM has effectively learned the
Aave V2 liquidation mechanics from its pre-training corpus, or (b) the
relatively simple numerical structure of the task (``is HF near 1?'') makes
it easily solvable without deep domain-specific training.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Sequence modelling matters.}
The LSTM's consistent advantage over cross-sectional models (LR, XGB, MLP)
confirms that the temporal evolution of an account's health factor---not
just its current value---is informative.  An account trending from HF 3.0
to HF 1.5 over 14 days carries materially higher risk than one that has
maintained HF 1.5 stably, yet both snapshots produce identical feature
vectors for cross-sectional models.

\paragraph{The simplicity of the dominant signal.}
The near-linear performance of logistic regression and the strong zero-shot
LLM results both suggest that the dominant predictive signal in this dataset
is relatively simple: proximity to the liquidation boundary.  The more
expressive models (XGB, MLP) do not substantially outperform LR, possibly
because the dataset is not large enough or the class imbalance too severe
to reliably learn higher-order interaction effects.

\paragraph{Behavioural signals.}
The elevated Cox hazard ratio of \texttt{repay\_borrow\_ratio} and the
positive SHAP contribution of this feature in XGBoost represent a
non-trivial, behaviour-based risk signal that could be exploited by DeFi
risk monitoring systems.  Accounts that repay aggressively without
subsequent new borrows may be signalling imminent distress.

\paragraph{LLM as a training-free baseline.}
The zero-shot LLM result challenges the assumption that labelled data are
strictly necessary for DeFi risk prediction.  In practice, a zero-shot LLM
could serve as a risk monitor in the absence of historical liquidation data,
for example during the launch of a new DeFi protocol where no training data
exists.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Several limitations merit acknowledgement.  First, the dataset lacks
supply-side data for 2021-Q1 through 2021-Q3 due to API restrictions;
this gap may cause the model to miss patterns from the early DeFi boom
period.  Second, the LLM evaluation uses a stratified sample, making PR-AUC
and F1 incomparable to supervised-model values.  Third, the survival
analysis computes hazard ratios on a pooled (all-accounts, full-history)
sample rather than the same chronological test set used by classifiers;
the C-index and classifier ROC-AUC are thus not directly aligned metrics.
Fourth, features are engineered from on-chain data only; off-chain
information (e.g.\ borrower identity, aggregate portfolio exposure) is not
available and could improve prediction.


% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

We have presented a systematic benchmark of seven modelling paradigms for
predicting short-horizon liquidation risk in the Aave V2 DeFi lending
protocol.  Our main findings are:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
    \item The LSTM is the strongest supervised model (ROC-AUC 0.809 / 0.825),
    demonstrating the value of temporal sequence modelling for on-chain risk.
    \item A zero-shot LLM predictor achieves comparable ROC-AUC (0.814 / 0.787)
    without any labelled training data, suggesting that large language models
    encode sufficient financial reasoning to serve as training-free baselines.
    \item Logistic regression with Platt calibration is a competitive and
    interpretable baseline, outperforming both XGBoost and MLP and providing
    well-calibrated probability estimates.
    \item SHAP attribution and Cox regression consistently identify
    \texttt{approx\_health\_factor} and \texttt{distance\_to\_liq} as the
    dominant risk factors, with \texttt{repay\_borrow\_ratio} and
    \texttt{vol\_times\_debt} providing complementary behavioural and
    market-interaction signals.
    \item The stratified Cox model (C-index 0.691) reveals that
    \texttt{repay\_borrow\_ratio} carries the largest estimated hazard
    ratio (1.30) among time-varying covariates---a nuanced signal invisible
    to horizon-classification models.
\end{enumerate}

These results collectively demonstrate that liquidation risk in DeFi lending
is predictable from publicly available on-chain state, across a range of
modelling approaches that vary substantially in their data requirements and
computational complexity.


% ============================================================
\section{Future Work and Limitations}
\label{sec:future}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Multi-protocol generalisation.}
The present study focuses exclusively on Aave V2 on Ethereum mainnet.
A natural extension is to evaluate whether models trained on Aave V2
transfer to Aave V3 (which introduces supply caps and efficiency modes),
Compound V3, or cross-chain deployments on Polygon and Arbitrum.  The
divergent collateral risk parameters across these protocols may require
protocol-specific feature engineering.

\paragraph{Real-time and continuous-time monitoring.}
Our models operate on daily snapshots, which may be too coarse for protocols
with rapid price movements.  A continuous-time model---such as a neural
temporal point process or a deep survival network---could process each
on-chain transaction as it occurs and update liquidation probability in near
real time.  Neural survival models such as DeepHit
\citep{lee2018deephit} or SurvTRACE \citep{wang2022survtrace} represent
promising directions.

\paragraph{Fine-tuned LLM predictor.}
The zero-shot LLM achieves strong rank-ordering but poor probability
calibration.  Fine-tuning a smaller language model on historical liquidation
data, combined with retrieval-augmented generation to provide relevant market
context at inference time, could improve both calibration and absolute
accuracy while retaining the interpretability advantages of language-based
reasoning.

\paragraph{Incorporating oracle and liquidation-bot dynamics.}
Liquidations in practice are mediated by MEV bots that compete to execute
profitable liquidation transactions.  The timing of liquidations therefore
depends not only on the protocol's liquidation threshold but on the
profitability of the liquidation call to external actors.  Incorporating
oracle update frequency and bot competition dynamics into the model could
reduce false positives near the liquidation boundary.

\paragraph{Class imbalance at the boundary.}
Our models are trained on all accounts with non-trivial positions; however,
the vast majority of training samples are far from the liquidation boundary
and contribute little predictive signal.  Curriculum learning or
importance weighting that focuses training on near-boundary accounts could
improve sensitivity at the risk horizon most relevant to borrowers and risk
managers.

\paragraph{Portfolio-level risk aggregation.}
The current model treats accounts independently.  In practice, a risk
manager may hold positions across multiple wallets or protocols.
Extending the model to aggregate risk across correlated accounts---for
example, via a graph neural network over co-deposited collateral assets---could
provide a more complete view of systemic exposure.

\paragraph{Missing data period.}
As noted in Section~\ref{sec:data}, supply-side data for 2021-Q1 through
2021-Q3 are unavailable due to Dune API restrictions.  Recovering this
period---either through a premium API plan or by direct archive-node
querying---would allow the model to learn from Aave's earliest
high-liquidation episodes, potentially improving generalisation to extreme
market conditions.
